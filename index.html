How It Works

Most Section 423 ESPPs let you purchase company stock at a 15% discount from the market price. If your plan has a lookback provision, you get the discount applied to the lower of the stock price at the offering start date or the purchase date.

Example: If the stock was $100 at the start and $150 at purchase, your purchase price with lookback is 85% × $100 = $85 (not 85% × $150 = $127.50). You're buying $150 stock for $85 — a 43% immediate gain.

Without lookback, your purchase price is always 85% of the end-of-period price. The discount is still valuable, but lookback provides additional upside when the stock has risen.

The IRS limits Section 423 ESPP purchases to $25,000 of fair market value per calendar year. The critical detail: FMV is measured at the offering start price, not the discounted purchase price.

Key insight: The limit is based on the stock price when the offering STARTED, not what you paid. A higher start price consumes your $25K capacity faster.

This means the number of shares you can acquire depends on the start price:

High start price ($400):  $25,000 ÷ $400 = 62.5 shares max
Low start price  ($150):  $25,000 ÷ $150 = 166.7 shares max

If your contributions would purchase more than the limit allows, the excess is refunded to you.

When an offering spans two calendar years (e.g., Dec 2025 → Jun 2026), it can draw from both years' $25K buckets. The ordering rule requires filling the earlier year's bucket first.

┌─── 2025 [$25K] ───┐    ┌─── 2026 [$25K] ───┐
│                    │    │                    │
│ ◄── Jun–Dec '25    │    │                    │
│ ◄── Dec–Jun '25-26─┼──►│ ◄── overflow       │
│                    │    │ ◄── Jun–Dec '26    │
└────────────────────┘    └────────────────────┘

This means a Dec→Jun offering shares capacity with the preceding Jun→Dec offering in the first year, and with the following Jun→Dec offering in the second year. Planning across offerings matters.

Ordering rule: When a cross-year offering allocates FMV, it fills the earliest calendar year first. Only after that year's $25K is exhausted does overflow go to the next year.

Most plans allow you to withdraw up to ~5 business days before the purchase date. This creates a useful strategy:

Always enroll. Enrolling preserves your optionality — you can always withdraw later. Not enrolling forfeits the opportunity entirely.

This calculator is most valuable ~1 week before the purchase date, when you have the most information about whether to stay in or withdraw. By then you know the current stock price and can estimate your final purchase price.

If the stock has dropped below the offering start price and your plan has no lookback, withdrawing and re-enrolling in the next offering at the lower start price may be advantageous.
